The year 1959 was a good one for motion pictures in the United States. _Rio Bravo_, _Imitation of Life_, _Sleeping Beauty_, _The Diary of Anne Frank_, _The Horse Soldiers_, _The Nun’s Story_, _Anatomy of a Murder_, _North by Northwest_, _Odds against Tomorrow_, _Pillow Talk_, _A Summer Place_, _On the Beach_, _Ben-Hur_ and _Suddenly, Last Summer_ were all released during that calendar year. Standing at the head of the pack was Billy Wilder’s rapid-fire comedy, _Some Like It Hot_, the one film from 1959 that most people remember today, if not for its witty dialogue, drag humour and carefully structured comic narrative, then for the luminous performance of its iconic female star, Marilyn Monroe. As Wilder recalled, ‘_Some Like It Hot_ will be a picture of mine people will see for as long as prints last, not because of me, but because of Miss Monroe.’

To be sure, 1959 also saw art-house releases in the US of films from other countries aimed at more discerning audiences, such as _Room at the Top_ from Britain, _The 400 Blows_ from France and _Wild Strawberries_ (1957) from Sweden. But another interesting aspect of American cinema in 1959, as Ed Sikov has noted, is that ‘for the first and only time in the decade, the four most commercially successful films of the year were comedies’: _Auntie Mame_ (1958), _The Shaggy Dog_, _Some Like It Hot_ and _Pillow Talk_.

Of the four comedies, _Some Like It Hot_, with its frenetic plot about two musicians who, after witnessing the St Valentine’s Day massacre, escape the gangsters by passing as women to join an all-female band, was the most daring. Compare it with _The Shaggy Dog_, which opened nationally at the same time. This film set the template for successive family comedies from Walt Disney starring Fred MacMurray (and MacMurray’s befuddled ‘dad’ persona would be severely undercut by Billy Wilder the following year with _The Apartment_). The other two comedies pushed the needle much further, although not as far as Wilder’s did. _Auntie Mame_ challenged the Eisenhower era’s conformity with Rosalind Russell’s madcap eponymous character and is now considered a camp classic. _Pillow Talk_ cunningly toyed with what had become the archetypal swinging bachelor figure (played by Rock Hudson) from _Playboy_ magazine, just as it peeled back the outwardly cold façade of the single career woman (played by Doris Day) to reveal her comparable heat. It does not take away from the considerable fun and wit of those two comedies to appreciate the greater seriousness of the generic and gender playfulness of _Some Like It Hot_. This tantalising oxymoron – can a film at the same time be serious and playful? – is at the heart of Billy Wilder’s genius as a writer and director, and the basis of _Some Like It Hot_’s enduring appeal, the legend of Miss Monroe notwithstanding.

Yet despite its wildly popular reception and Academy Award recognition with half a dozen nominations, including ones for director Wilder and star Jack Lemmon, _Some Like It Hot_ was not without a fair amount of notoriety at the time of its release. Wilder was able to avoid interference from the Production Code Administration (PCA) but shortly before its opening the Catholic Legion of Decency almost condemned _Some Like It Hot_, according to correspondence in the PCA file. Ultimately rating it as ‘morally objectionable’, the Legion complained about the cross-dressing, smutty dialogue and offensive costumes. United Artists (UA), which released the film for the Mirisch Company, had some difficulty with local censorship boards, too. The board in the state of Kansas wanted to cut the yacht scene in which Tony Curtis seduces Monroe but UA refused, although viewers in Kansas City, Kansas, could drive across the state line to see the uncut film in the larger sister city in Missouri. Ultimately, UA permitted 105 feet to be removed from the scene and the film got to play in the state of Kansas after all. In Memphis, Tennessee, to give another example, the film was restricted to adults.

Trouble was already on the horizon even before the film opened as an Easter attraction in the spring of 1959. The first sneak preview at a neighbourhood theatre in Los Angeles was a disaster; everyone was silent, presumably, it was thought, because the regular feature showing that evening did not adequately prepare the audience to watch a boisterous comedy. Studio execs wanted Wilder to recut the film but he only removed a snippet, a short scene that anticlimactically concluded the train sequence. A second preview was arranged at the Village Theatre in Westwood, close to the UCLA campus, and this time, to prepare audiences for what was to come, instead of following the custom of proclaiming a major studio preview, the sign as the screening began read: ‘THE MANAGEMENT IS PROUD TO PRESENT A MINOR FEATURE PREVIEW.’ The audience at this preview howled from start to finish.

Decades later, accounts in some books and articles have perpetuated confusion about the film’s immediate box-office success, its critical reception and even its premiere date. Two people directly involved in the production, I. A. L. Diamond and Tony Curtis, and two of Wilder’s biographers, Charlotte Chandler and Gene Phillips, claim that the film opened to great reviews and muted business at first, but the opposite was the case. While the Hollywood trades and some weekly magazines raved about the comedy, many major newspaper reviews were mixed. In the _Chicago Daily Tribune_, Mae Tinee complained about its crude humour; in the _New York Times_, A. H. Weiler griped that its two-hour running time was too long for a comedy; and in the _Los Angeles Times_, Philip Scheuer was irritated by almost everything, from the generic blending to Monroe’s zoftig figure to Curtis’s Cary Grant accent. In the _Washington Post_, Richard Coe, on the other hand, raved about the film, calling it a ‘rollicking blockbuster’. He advised his readers that they would want to see it more than once.

While its notoriety as well as its popularity persists, having risen to mythic proportions in Monroe’s case, the secure position of _Some Like It Hot_ in the canon of great American cinema is by now unquestioned. In 1989, it was among the first group of films selected for the National Film Registry by the Library of Congress. It placed fourteenth on the American Film Institute’s list of ‘100 Years ... 100 Movies’ in 1998 and first on its list of ‘100 Years ... 100 Laughs’ in 2000. In a 2017 poll by the BBC, _Some Like It Hot_ was voted the best comedy of all time. In 2022, it ranked 38th in _Sight and Sound_’s critics’ poll of the greatest films of all time.

Such high esteem is well deserved. _Some Like It Hot_ was Wilder’s second collaboration with I. A. L. (‘Izzy’) Diamond, who would serve as his writing partner and associate producer for his subsequent movies. When writing the film in 1958 with Diamond, Wilder recalled, ‘We were pretty sure it would be a good comedy. What we did not know was that it would be a great comedy.’ Yet once it was released, they must have sensed that they had made something great. As Diamond’s wife Barbara recalled, ‘_Some Like It Hot_ is the one picture that both of them said they wouldn’t change a line if they had to do it over. And this was from men who second-guessed themselves for years after a picture of theirs was out of their hands.’
