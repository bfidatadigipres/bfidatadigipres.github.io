**A contemporary review**  
As an abstract of themes, images and motifs from classic American and French _film noir_, John Woo’s film makes Walter Hill’s _The_ _Driver_ look like Ken Loach. Woo began directing in 1973 and has soldiered his way through most Hong Kong genres, from kung-fu to Cantonese opera, but his career rocketed into overdrive when he joined Tsui Hark’s Film Workshop in 1986 and made  _A_ _Better_ _Tomorrow_, which became the highest-grossing film in Hong Kong history. Tsui prevailed on him to make _A Better Tomorrow II_, but he refused to make the third film in the series (actually a prequel to the other two, eventually directed by Tsui Hark himself) and turned to _The_ _Killer_ instead. Like all other Film Workshop productions (cf. _A Chinese Ghost Story_), Woo’s recent films are steeped in the company’s house style: hyper-kinetic pacing, high-gloss cinematography and regular injections of stunts and/or special effects. Unlike his colleagues, though, Woo has managed to resist ‘creative interference’ with his work; _A Better Tomorrow_ and _The_ _Killer_ may owe their hyperbolic action scenes to company requirements, but they are probably the only Film Workshop movies to date not recast, reshot, recut or redirected by Tsui Hark.

_The_ _Killer_ is inescapably a self-conscious commentary on its own genre. Like a Leone Western, it foregrounds all its generic points of reference without apology or embarrassment. Woo told Hong Kong interviewers last year that he originally intended the film as a homage to Martin Scorsese and Jean-Pierre Melville: hence the name ‘Jeff’ (from _Le_ _Samourai_), the sensual, quasi-erotic treatment of male bonding, the emphasis on underworld codes of honour, the fragments of world-weary dialogue about feeling out of time and out of place, the conflation of Catholic and gangster elements (the decision to stage the climactic siege in a church, complete with candles, white doves and an exploding Madonna, is more ‘Scorsese’ than Scorsese himself) and much besides. In the United States – where it will probably be Hong Kong’s first ‘crossover’ success since the heyday of Bruce Lee, if it survives the handicap of an ‘X’ rating – critics have tended to see _The_ _Killer_ in these terms exclusively, as a phenomenal reworking of Hollywood models: ‘_Magnificent_ _Obsession_ remade by Sam Peckinpah,’ in Jim Hoberman’s memorable phrase.

What Woo’s American fans have missed, doubtless because they failed to pick up on Hong Kong genre cinema last time around, are the film’s equally deep roots in the Chinese _wuxia_ _pian_ (‘martial chivalry’ genre) of the 1960s. The film harks back with overwhelming nostalgia to the swordplay epics of Woo’s adolescence. The Wang Yu character in Zhang Che’s _Golden_ _Swallow_ (1968) is a virtual role model for Chow Yun-fat’s Jeff: exquisitely tailored, stoic in adversity, disdainful of anaesthetic when treated for wounds, and given to slaughtering literally dozens of (deserving) foes whenever challenged. Wang Yu did not condescend to play men with friends, male or female, until well into the 1970s, but his director Zhang Che went on from _Golden_ _Swallow_ to specialise in intense male-bonding movies, always involving violent death – including _The Blood Brothers_ (1973), on which John Woo worked as assistant director. It is hardly a coincidence that while Woo was busy updating these all-but-gay myths into gangster terms, his contemporary Ke Xingpei was directing a remake of _Golden_ _Swallow_ itself. After nearly two decades of the crude martial Darwinism found in kung-fu movies and their spin-offs, Hong Kong culture is clearly hankering after the more chivalrous days of heroes who ‘act like they have a dream’, as Lee here describes Jeff.

It is probably this very nostalgia that saves _The_ _Killer_ from the innate hollowness of most genre commentaries. The film is, in every conceivable sense, full-blooded. It has an almost flawless thriller plot (no holes, no glaring implausibilities, no hard-to-take coincidences), but wastes no time on establishing or bridging scenes and keeps expository dialogue to the barest minimum. It is mounted as a rhapsody, complete with languorous dissolves and tightly integrated spasms of slow motion. It centres on ultra-charismatic performances from Chow Yun-fat (widely recognised as Hong Kong’s finest actor) and Danny Lee (a one-time stalwart of Shaw Brothers schlock, here liberated by his ambiguous role), but is never shy of subordinating them to the needs of the visual rhetoric. Even the steady escalation of the violence – by the end, the corpse count has reached three figures and thousands of rounds of ammunition have been fired – is slightly muted by the mood of romantic fatalism that drenches the proceedings, enhanced by the elegiac score commissioned from Lowell Lowe. In short, the film works to earn its genre stripes. In its serenely overstated way, it is as resonant and emotive as the ‘classics’ it refers to.

Two sequences in particular merit a place in genre anthologies of the future, both designed to explore the fundamental similarity between criminal and cop. One is the meeting of Jeff and Lee in Jennie’s apartment; the two men, with cocked handguns at each other’s heads, pretend to be childhood chums while the unsuspecting blind woman bustles between them, trying to serve them tea. The other is the calm before the storm in the church, where the sweet nothings exchanged by Jeff and Lee are punctuated by flash cutaways to Sydney’s fantastically brutal beating at the hands of Weng; the conjunction of male romance and male sadomasochism lifts a characteristic trait of the genre to a new high. Since completing _The_ _Killer_, which did only half as well as _A_ _Better_ _Tomorrow_ at the Hong Kong box-office, Woo has left Film Workshop and formed his own company. His inaugural production _Bullet in the Head_ is taking a worryingly long time to bring to completion; its release date has already been postponed three times. It may yet turn out that _The_ _Killer_ marks a watershed in his career: a rhapsodic genre commentary than cannot be capped.  
**Tony Rayns, _Monthly Film Bulletin_, September 1990**
