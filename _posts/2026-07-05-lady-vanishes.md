On a train crossing Europe on the brink of the Second World War, Iris takes tea with the friendly English governess she met in her compartment. Minutes later, the governess has vanished and none of the train’s eccentric gallery of passengers will admit to having ever seen her. Propelled by a superb Launder and Gilliat script, Hitchcock’s finest British film is, by turns, hilarious, thrilling, romantic and scary.  
**Kazuo Ishiguro**

This was a railway coupling made in dreamtime. In Alfred Hitchcock’s _The Lady Vanishes_, Margaret Lockwood and Michael Redgrave, playing a London socialite and her admirer on a trans-European express carrying a smorgasbord of Axis conspirators, brought a sexy screwball frisson to the staid British cinema of the late 1930s. England may have been ‘on the brink’, but the blinkered English weren’t prepared to let imminent war divert them from their passions. Two quintessential Englishmen, Charters (Basil Radford) and Caldicott (Naunton Wayne), fret about making it to a Manchester Test Match on time. The musical folklorist Gilbert (Redgrave) frets about making it with Iris Henderson (Lockwood) before she marries for money a few days hence. Iris frets about Miss Froy (Dame May Whitty), the tweedy governess whose disappearance shortly after departure indicates foul play.

It’s tempting to seek topicality in the appeasement theme, the espionage plot and the film’s meditation on Englishness, but none of these elements resonates much today. What does is Hitchcock’s manipulation of Iris’ consciousness and, through the use of the train as a vehicle for dreaming, the idea that she, not Miss Froy (the literal disappearee), is the lady who vanishes. Then there’s her banter with Gilbert – the kind of droll to-and-fro sadly missing from contemporary comedy. Back projection, process shots, transparencies, archival footage, railway noises and wisps of steam drifting through an open window give a semblance of realism to the train itself – probably the Arlberg Orient Express – but modern audiences understand that it never existed outside a sound stage.

Launder and Gilliat adapted _The Lady Vanishes_ from Ethel Lina White’s tepid 1936 novel _The Wheel Spins_, which had followed Graham Greene’s _Stamboul Train_ and Agatha Christie’s _Murder on the Orient Express_ into bookstores. For all its low-key visual artifice, it’s the most iconic entry in the British train-film cycle of the 1930s – in cinematic terms, the golden age of steam. _The Lady Vanishes_ is cosmopolitan and predicated on speed – of narrative, wit, motion and emotion.

The advent of talkies and the glamour of continental rail travel, which was being democratised in the early 1930s, may explain the vogue for train movies in the manner of _Boule de suif_, Guy de Maupassant’s archetypal travel short story about a group of disparate characters reacting to a crisis. Such films include Walter Forde’s _Rome Express_ (1932), co-written by Gilliat, and Paul Martin’s _Orient Express_ (1933), based on _Stambool Train_. The Hollywood exemplar is _Shanghai Express_ (1932), filmed by Josef von Stemberg at Paramount and on a stretch of the Santa Fe line with a thousand Asian extras; for _The Lady Vanishes_ Hitchcock had to make do with a 90-foot set on a small stage at Islington Studios.

Gilliat also contributed dialogue to the 1931 film of _The Ghost Train_, directed by Forde, as was the 1941 remake. There was the 1935 thriller _Night Mail_, and Harry Watt and Basil Wright’s lyrical 1936 GPO documentary of the same name. Launder and Gilliat first teamed to write _Seven Sinners_ (1936), which was based on Arnold Ridley and Bernard Merivale’s play _The Wrecker,_ and recycled spectacular train footage from a 1928 film version.

That Hitchcock and Launder-Gilliat were independently enamoured of railway settings was auspicious for their collaboration on _The Lady Vanishes_. But it nearly didn’t happen. Launder brought White’s novel to Gainsborough Pictures in 1936. The American Roy William Neill signed on to direct Launder and Gilliat’s adaptation and a second unit was dispatched to Yugoslavia. The production was closed down when the authorities in Belgrade objected to the script. When Hitchcock inherited it in 1937, he suggested the hotel opening and the climactic shoot-out, which Gilliat worked in. ‘I think this is the only case of Hitchcock taking over a script completed for another director,’ he said.

But Hitchcock was not the film’s sole auteur. ‘Matters of ascription are far from simple,’ Bruce Babington notes in his book on the writers, citing how the clue leading to the unmasking of the police chief in their _Seven Sinners_ screenplay was lifted from the revelation of the arch-criminal in _The 39 Steps_. Launder and Gilliat and Charles Bennett, writer or co-writer of the five Hitchcock films before _The Lady Vanishes_, were incensed when _The_ _New Yorker_ reported in 1938 that the director was responsible for ‘99.4 per cent’ of his pictures. The screenplays of _The Lady Vanishes_ and Carol Reed’s _Night Train to Munich_, Babington says, ‘are highly structured prototypes for the directors marked indelibly by the wit and ethos of the writers. Even such definitively ‘Hitchcockian’ elements as the oneiric episodes unleashed by the flowerpot falling on Iris’ head are present in the scenario, even if we feel that Hitchcock worked them in ways that were highly characteristic.’

It’s clear that the sparkling screwball interaction between Iris and Gilbert was, verbally at least, the work of Launder and Gilliat, seeded as it was in _Rome Express_ and _Seven Sinners_. The elegant repartee they wrote for Edmund Lowe and Constance Cummings in _Seven Sinners_ was modelled on the romantic sparring in _The Thin Man_ and, presumably, _It Happened One Night_ (1934) and _Twentieth_ _Century_ (1934), the most acerbic of Hollywood train movies. The intertextual relationship between _The 39 Steps_ and _The Lady Vanishes_ doesn’t simplify matters since, as Babington asserts, ‘Launder and Gilliat’s relationship with Hitchcock was a complex, lingering one, involving mutual debts and borrowings.’ Hitchcock’s signature is indelible, however. The films share the Hitchcockian MacGuffin of a coded tune. Iris’ suggestion that a scream she and Gilbert hear maybe a train whistle hearkens to the match cut in _The 39 Steps_. Gilbert, like Hannay, climbs from one compartment to another outside the train. And his quick impersonations echo Hannay’s use of different identities – including milkman, mechanic and politician.

Hitchcock, of course, was responsible for the rhythmic flow of the film, the note-perfect performances and the pleasing shifts in mood. All this was absent from Launder and Gilliat’s _Night Train to Munich_, a leaden thriller about a British agent impersonating a Nazi officer in which director Carol Reed squanders both the train sequence and Margaret Lockwood, cast as the daughter of a German scientist. Not even the return of Charters and Caldicott, with their dry public-school badinage, can save it. Effortlessly appealing, Gilbert and Iris – who plan to marry and avoid trains – should have returned like _The Thin Man’s_ Nick and Nora Charles with their own mystery franchise, but it wasn’t to be. The last word on _The Lady Vanishes_ should go to François Truffaut. ‘They show it often in Paris; sometimes I see it twice in one week,’ he told Hitchcock, as if nothing were more natural.  
**Graham Fuller, _Sight and Sound_, January 2008**
