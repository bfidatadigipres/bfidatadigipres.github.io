Two convicts break out of a hellish prison to find themselves trapped on a speeding train they can’t control. Is this Jon Voight’s finest moment? (He and Roberts both received Oscar nominations.) On the surface very American, this superb edge-of-the-seat existentialist parable was directed by Russian Andrei Konchalovsky and based on an unproduced Akira Kurosawa screenplay.  
**Kazuo Ishiguro**

**A contemporary review**  
On a visit to California in 1975, the year he was recruited by Mosfilm to direct his most famous work, the epic _Siberiade_, Andrei Konchalovsky told _Variety_ that he did not feel he could make a film in the West. ‘I am spoiled by having been able to work under our conditions, without economic pressures. In the Soviet Union, films are produced without a constant eye on their commercial success. That may in part explain the slowness, the lack of suspense in a Western sense, that is sometimes found in our films.’ Five years later, the Soviet Union having granted him permission to work abroad without closing the door behind him (he has a blameless artistic pedigree and his father, Sergei Mikhalkov, is a leading member of the Union of Soviet Writers), Konchalovsky arrived in Hollywood looking for backers. ‘There are different kinds of monkey. Curious monkey and monkey who is not curious. Monkey who is curious go from the forest to the savanna.’

Konchalovsky found a friend in the Cannon Group, and although his first American film, the elegiac _Maria’s Lovers_ (completed in October 1983, but not released in the U.S. until January 1985, and then with no clear strategy), failed at the box office, the famously tight-budgeted Golan and Globus both liked it and were eager to work with Konchalovsky again. He was signed by Cannon for a series of projects, beginning with the ‘action adventure’ _Runaway Train_, a film as different in pace, tone and subject matter from _Maria’s Lovers_ as, one imagines, Godard’s _King Lear_ (forthcoming from Cannon) will be from the _Popsicle_ cycle. The savanna is full of surprises.

_Runaway Train_, which is set in that former spur of the Russian Empire, Alaska, ends with a quote from _Richard III_: ‘No beast so fierce but knows some touch of pity.’ It has about it a good deal of what might be termed Hollywood Russia, notably a large dose of undiluted symbolism. The last shot bangs the message home (the story is Kurosawa’s, but he would have done it differently): the stigmatised Manny, arms outstretched, balanced on top of the pounding locomotive, headed for the jaws of Hell (or Heaven). The snowy wastes might be Siberian: Konchalovsky claims the idea for his true Siberian epic first struck him on a flight across America, looking down on the variety of landscapes below. And the empty ‘Russian’ vastness of the landscape is caught, in particular, by one beautiful long shot of the train, in the foreground of which a family of moose are glimpsed lumbering through the snow. The film is marked too by a sort of grandiose fatalism popularly associated with Dostoevsky and here carried through with a wholly un-American seriousness of purpose.

In other respects, however, the epitaph and the symbolism aside, _Runaway Train_ is very much an all-action Cannon yarn. Unlike his fellow expatriate Tarkovsky (whose _Andrei Rublev_ he co-scripted), Konchalovsky has shown himself more than adept at serving up a certain kind of Western product. Beginning with a tingling credit sequence, an explosion of beautifully edited violence at the ringside, and then a cunning escape (the bars that have to be prised apart are in a sewage pipe), _Runaway Train_ is an exemplary demonstration, executed with great gusto, of how to banish slowness and pump up suspense. It is also, it must be said, an example of a certain kind of Hollywood silliness. Warden Ranken is all brooding vengefulness; Manny, the burnt-out lag who knows it’s too late to remake his life, can still solemnly scold admiring young Buck for wanting to copy him; Sara, a freckled tomboy with ragdoll bunches and heart-shaped earrings, is the complete plucky girl; and so on. The characters are all pretty much cut from cardboard and, given the plot, are no worse for that.

But Konchalovsky is by no means a one-note director. He balances the scenes in the train with those, pitched quite differently, in the control room; and there is a nicely judged thread of comedy running through the latter (a dreamy, voluptuous secretary, for instance, floats through the crisis sublimely unaware, apparently, of what is going on). There is, too, a moment of fortuitous melancholy: the young computer wizard is ruminating on the uselessness of his expensive equipment while in the background a television shows a perfect touchdown by the space shuttle – ‘... a picture-perfect mission, one that is always hoped for but rarely achieved.’ In a film in which the action was less well-crafted, and the spectator not being propelled relentlessly from scene to scene, this moment might have stopped the plot dead: that it does not is a considerable tribute to Konchalovsky and his editor Henry Richardson.

The film is handsomely photographed by Alan Hume, the predominant tone being a blue-grey metallic sheen; the prison scenes have a raw, nervy quality which makes one forget their familiarity; and, at the centre, Jon Voight as Manny (an impressive job of uglification by the make-up artist) has old-fashioned star presence courtesy of the Method School.  
**John Pym, _Monthly Film Bulletin_, June 1986**
